% ─────────────────────────── ABSTRACT ──────────────────────────────────────
\begin{center}
  \textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}

\noindent This paper estimates the causal effect of Epic Games Store free game giveaways on
Steam concurrent player counts for the same title. Using a staggered
difference-in-differences design with 446 treated games across 2018--2025, we exploit
variation in the \textit{timing} of each game's first Epic giveaway and compare four
complementary estimators: two-way fixed effects (TWFE), local projections DiD (LP-DiD),
stacked DiD \citep{cengiz2019}, and cohort-specific ATTs following \citet{callaway2021}. The central finding is a
pattern of short-run complementarity followed by medium-run substitution: in the month
of the giveaway, log Steam players rise by approximately 4.7\% (stacked DiD) to 13.1\%
(LP-DiD), consistent with the giveaway functioning as a marketing event. However, this
effect reverses quickly---already negative under stacked DiD at $k = 1$---and declines
significantly below pre-giveaway baseline through the entire one-year post-treatment
window, reaching roughly 10--20\% below baseline by month ten depending on estimator. The contemporaneous spike passes a randomization placebo test (empirical
$p<0.001$) and is robust to estimator choice. Repeat giveaways generate no additional
engagement ($\hat{\delta}\approx 0$, $p=0.999$). The results suggest that Epic giveaways
serve primarily as advertising that temporarily lifts Steam awareness, while the
free copy on a rival platform gradually substitutes Steam engagement over subsequent months.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{Keywords:} platform competition, digital games, difference-in-differences,
cross-platform spillovers, Epic Games Store, Steam

% ─────────────────────────── 1. INTRODUCTION ───────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

When most gamers hear about a new video game---whether through social media, a friend's
recommendation, or a news headline---the first instinct is to open Steam and search for it,
not the Epic Games Store, GOG, or any other platform. Steam is simply where games
\textit{live} in the average gamer's mental model, regardless of where they are sold or
given away. This behavior reflects the social ecosystem Steam has cultivated: friend
lists, community hubs, reviews, achievements, and wishlist notifications so comprehensive
that it functions not merely as a store but as the primary discovery and identity layer
for PC gaming. Even a game distributed entirely for free on a rival platform is liable
to drive curious players to \textit{Steam} first.

This intuition recently received vivid anecdotal support. In January 2026, Dave Oshry,
CEO of New Blood Interactive, publicly shared that when \textit{Blood West}---a cult
eldritch first-person shooter published on Steam by New Blood---was given away for
free on the Epic Games Store over the 2025 holiday period, Steam sales surged by
approximately 200\% on the same day \citep{oshry2026}. ``I used to think EGS was a
Marketing Black Hole,'' Oshry wrote, ``but turns out having your game be free on Epic is
great advertising for Steam sales!'' The comment went viral in the gaming community,
sparking a Reddit thread on r/Steam that reached hundreds of upvotes and generated
broad discussion about whether the Epic giveaway program systematically benefits Steam
engagement rather than draining it \citep{reddit2026}.

These observations raise a precise and economically interesting causal question: does an
Epic Games Store free giveaway \textit{increase} or \textit{decrease} engagement for
the same game on Steam? Standard substitution logic predicts a decrease---a zero-price
substitute on a rival platform should divert players away from Steam. Yet the anecdotal
evidence points in the opposite direction, at least in the short run. The giveaway
may function as an advertising shock: it generates media coverage, social media chatter,
and word-of-mouth that raises awareness of the title and drives traffic to the platform
where players have their existing library, social graph, and community history---Steam.

The PC gaming platform market is a compelling laboratory for this question. Steam,
operated by Valve and launched in 2003, remains overwhelmingly dominant with more than
120 million monthly active users and a catalogue exceeding 50{,}000 titles as of 2025.
Its competitive moat rests not only on library size but on thick social infrastructure:
forum discussions, user reviews, community guides, achievement tracking, and a friend-list
layer that creates switching costs no price cut can easily replicate. The Epic Games
Store (EGS), launched in December 2018 on the back of \textit{Fortnite}'s enormous
revenue, has pursued an aggressive free-game strategy as its primary differentiation
lever: since its launch, it has given away more than 540 games at no cost to users.
Other platforms---GOG (DRM-free, $\sim$6{,}000 titles), itch.io (indie-focused), Xbox
Game Pass (subscription), and console storefronts---occupy different market segments
and present less direct competition to Steam's mass-market general catalogue.
\citet{nabil2025} document that Steam significantly outperforms EGS on content variety,
ease of use, and timeliness in user-satisfaction surveys, suggesting that Epic's
zero-price promotions attract users without closing the platform quality gap.

Against this backdrop, this paper estimates the causal effect of EGS free giveaways on
Steam player engagement using a staggered difference-in-differences event study across
446 games given away between December 2018 and 2025. The identifying variation is the
\textit{timing} of each game's first EGS giveaway: games awaiting future giveaways
serve as controls for currently treated games, exploiting the rolling staggered rollout
of the Epic program rather than relying on an external never-treated comparison group.
Engagement is measured via monthly average concurrent players from SteamCharts.

The central empirical finding is a two-phase dynamic. In the month of the giveaway and
the month after, Steam player counts rise by approximately 10--13\%---a positive
cross-platform spillover consistent with the advertising mechanism. This effect, however,
fully reverses within two months of the giveaway and declines steadily below the
pre-giveaway baseline through the remaining one-year window, reaching roughly 27\%
below baseline by month twelve. The contemporaneous spike is statistically significant
($p<0.001$) and passes a randomization placebo test; the subsequent reversal is equally
significant and robust to alternative estimators. Repeat giveaways of the same game
generate essentially zero additional effect ($\hat{\delta}\approx 0$, $p=0.999$).

This pattern resolves the apparent tension between the advertising hypothesis and
substitution theory: both are correct, but they operate at different horizons. The
giveaway announcement attracts attention to the title on Steam (the platform of
primary discovery), generating a temporary engagement spike; but the free copy
distributed via Epic gradually substitutes Steam engagement as players migrate their
playtime to the platform on which they received the game.

The paper makes three main contributions. First, it provides a large-sample causal
estimate (446 games, 38{,}642 game-month observations, seven years of giveaway history)
of cross-platform spillovers from free game promotions, substantially expanding on
prior single-firm or case-study evidence. Second, it documents the full dynamic
trajectory of the effect---including the sustained post-giveaway reversal---which
prior work has not characterized. Third, it provides a clean null result for repeat
giveaways, with practical implications for platform curation strategy.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:lit} reviews
related literature. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data and panel construction.
Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results}
reports results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the findings and discusses
mechanisms. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes. The appendix contains additional
figures and robustness checks.

% ─────────────────────────── 2. LITERATURE REVIEW ──────────────────────────
\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:lit}

\subsection{Cross-Platform Spillovers and Platform Competition}

The most directly related work is \citet{clarkson2025}, a doctoral dissertation whose
second chapter quasi-experimentally studies cross-platform spillover effects of gaming
platform giveaways. Using Steam player traffic data, Clarkson finds that games given
away by a competing platform (Epic) see Steam traffic increase by more than 24\%.
He also documents that word-of-mouth and Steam review activity increase significantly
during these promotions, and that spillover effects are larger for games that leverage
Steam-exclusive features---friend lists, achievements, community hubs---inaccessible
on the competing platform. The dissertation's question is nearly identical in spirit
to this paper, and its findings provide direct empirical support for a positive
cross-platform spillover mechanism. However, Clarkson's analysis focuses on the
short-run contemporaneous effect; the present paper extends the analysis to the full
twelve-month post-treatment trajectory and documents the subsequent reversal below
baseline.

More broadly, the question fits into the literature on platform competition and
multi-homing in two-sided markets \citep{rochet2003, armstrong2006}. In two-sided
platforms, price cuts on one side (distributing games free to users) can strengthen
the platform's network if they attract more users and thereby make the platform more
attractive to developers. However, when two platforms primarily serve the same user
base---as Steam and EGS do for PC gamers---a free promotion on one platform may
simply reallocate engagement rather than expand the aggregate market. The time-series
evidence in this paper is consistent with initial market-expansion (aggregate awareness
rising) followed by platform-reallocation (players migrating to the free-copy platform).

\subsection{Network Effects and Social Multipliers on Gaming Platforms}

\citet{tudon2022} estimates the magnitude of direct network effects on Steam using
price elasticity comparisons between socially isolated and socially connected consumers.
He finds a 0.13\% own-demand increase per 1\% increase in friends' demand, and
counterfactual simulations suggest that targeted promotions to highly networked users
could raise platform-wide demand by approximately 5\%. This social-multiplier channel
provides a specific microeconomic mechanism for the advertising effect: by expanding
the addressable player community (anyone who now owns the game on Epic can be introduced
to the title by a Steam-playing friend), a free copy on a rival platform may activate
network complementarities that benefit the incumbent.

\subsection{Platform Satisfaction and Incumbency Advantage}

\citet{nabil2025} conduct a user-satisfaction survey comparing Steam and the Epic Games
Store using the End User Computing Satisfaction (EUCS) framework across 76 respondents.
They find that Steam significantly outperforms EGS on content variety, ease of use, and
timeliness, while no significant difference exists on accuracy or visual format. Their
finding that Epic's free game program attracts users without closing the satisfaction
gap is consistent with a world where Steam's incumbency advantage is durable---players
who obtain a game via Epic may continue to engage with Steam's ecosystem (reviews,
community hubs, friends) even if they play the game via Epic.

The observation that gamers treat Steam as their default discovery platform echoes the
insight from Adrian Chmielarz (creator of \textit{Painkiller} and \textit{Witchfire}):
``EGS is a shop, and Steam is a community. They don't have written reviews. They don't
have the forums. There's nothing to do there but to buy.'' \citep{frvr2026}. This
functional asymmetry---Steam as a social community, EGS as a transactional storefront---
may explain why even free distribution via EGS can generate Steam traffic: the social
activity associated with a game (discussion, reviews, achievement hunting) predominantly
occurs on Steam regardless of which platform delivered the copy.

\subsection{Staggered Difference-in-Differences Methodology}

The methodological backbone of the empirical strategy draws on \citet{callaway2021},
who establish that staggered treatment designs require cohort-specific estimands and
not-yet-treated control groups to avoid the negative-weighting bias that afflicts
standard TWFE panel regressions when treatment effects are heterogeneous across
adoption cohorts. \citet{dube2023} develop a local projections approach to DiD
event studies (LP-DiD) that is robust to heterogeneous treatment effects by restricting
each period-$k$ comparison to not-yet-treated controls. \citet{cengiz2019} introduce
stacked difference-in-differences, constructing cohort-specific balanced panels with
not-yet-treated controls and stacking them to obtain an event-study estimator free
of negative weights. We use CS, LP-DiD, and stacked DiD as robustness checks on the
standard TWFE estimator.

% ─────────────────────────── 3. DATA ───────────────────────────────────────
\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Epic Giveaway History}

We obtained a Kaggle dataset \citep{dongre2025} recording every free game offered through
the Epic Games Store from December 2018 through 2025---541 giveaway events corresponding
to 471 unique game titles. The remaining 70 events are repeat appearances. The source
dataset flags repeat appearances with a ``(repeat)'' suffix appended to the game name.
Nine titles had this suffix incorrectly applied to their \textit{first} appearance;
these have been corrected so that every title's earliest entry is flag-free and only
subsequent giveaways of the same title carry the suffix.

Repeats cluster in two distinct patterns: \textit{distant repeats}, where a game is
offered again more than twelve months after its first appearance (e.g., \textit{Kingdom
Come: Deliverance} at 57-month spacing), and \textit{proximate repeats}, where the same
game is offered within a few months or even days of the first---most notably during
Epic's annual holiday giveaway batches in December 2019, 2020, 2022, and 2023.
Table~\ref{tab:epic_csv} summarizes the giveaway structure. We retain \textit{all}
giveaway dates for each game to support the multi-event analysis.

\subsection{SteamCharts Monthly Player Data}

For each Epic giveaway title matched to a Steam App ID, we scraped monthly average and
peak concurrent player counts from \texttt{steamcharts.com/app/\{appid\}}. SteamCharts
renders its historical tables server-side, so standard HTTP requests suffice; we imposed
a one-second rate limit per request. The overall panel spans July 2012 through February
2026, but each game's time series begins only from the month it first appeared on
SteamCharts (typically near its Steam release date). Coverage varies substantially
across titles: the median game has 88 months of data, 27 games have fewer than 24
months, and 12 have fewer than 12 months.

\subsection{Merge Pipeline}

Matching Epic game names to Steam App IDs requires reconciling inconsistent titling
(subtitles, punctuation, edition suffixes). We used RapidFuzz fuzzy string matching with
a WRatio threshold of 88, which auto-matched 413 of 471 unique names; the remaining 58
received manual review, of which 50 were resolved to a valid Steam App ID and 8 were
confirmed absent from Steam (5 Epic exclusives and 3 placeholder entries).
Table~\ref{tab:pipeline} summarizes the pipeline. Fifteen matched entries are DLC packs
or in-game content releases mapped to the base game's Steam App ID; these are retained
but the measurement mismatch is discussed as a limitation.

\subsection{Final Panel and Summary Statistics}

The assembled dataset contains 38,642 game-month observations across 446 games.
The outcome variable is $\ln(\texttt{avg\_players}+1)$, where \texttt{avg\_players}
is the SteamCharts monthly average concurrent player count. The $+1$ offset handles
months with zero reported players (common for low-traffic titles); the log transformation
compresses the heavy right tail driven by a small number of AAA titles.

Table~\ref{tab:sumstats} presents summary statistics. The raw player counts are highly
right-skewed: the mean monthly average of 1{,}584 players is more than 24 times the
median of 65 players, reflecting a distribution dominated by long-tail indie titles
with occasional blockbusters (e.g., GTA V, Destiny 2). The log-transformed outcome is
far better behaved (mean 4.43, SD 2.36). The repeat-event indicator is 1 in only 2\%
of game-month observations, reflecting that most games were given away exactly once.

Figure~\ref{fig:outcome} (left panel) shows overlaid density histograms of the
log-transformed outcome for pre-giveaway ($k < 0$) and post-giveaway ($k \geq 0$)
months. The post-giveaway distribution is shifted slightly rightward on average, but
the distributions are broadly similar in shape, with the heavy near-zero mass
reflecting the 101 games with a pre-treatment median below 10 concurrent players.
The right panel plots the unconditional mean of the outcome at each event-time
$k \in [-12, 12]$: the pre-period mean is approximately flat, providing preliminary
support for parallel trends, and a sharp uptick is visible at $k = 0$, with partial
persistence into $k = 1$ before declining.

Figure~\ref{fig:desc} provides three additional panels characterizing the panel's
structure. Panel (a) shows a roughly bimodal pre-treatment distribution of log players
across games---a large cluster of low-traffic titles (fewer than 20 median players)
and a smaller cluster of popular ones. Panel (b) shows that treatment cohorts are
broadly evenly distributed across 2019--2023, providing the timing variation needed
for staggered DiD identification. Panel (c) shows that approximately 10\% of games
in most cohort years have a proximate repeat giveaway (second event within 12 months).

% ─────────────────────────── 4. EMPIRICAL METHOD ───────────────────────────
\section{Empirical Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Identification Strategy}

The identifying variation is the \textit{timing} of each game's first Epic giveaway
across 80 distinct calendar months spanning December 2018 through late 2025. Because
the Epic program has cycled continuously through a broad catalogue of genres and
release-years, games awaiting future giveaways serve as natural controls for currently
treated games. This ``not-yet-treated'' design avoids reliance on an external pool of
games that were never part of the Epic giveaway program and therefore potentially
systematically different.

The key identifying assumption is parallel trends: in the absence of treatment, the
log player trajectory of games in different giveaway cohorts would have followed the
same path after conditioning on game and calendar-month fixed effects. This assumption
is partially testable via pre-treatment event-study coefficients and is more plausible
to the extent that Epic's curation team does not systematically select games at
particular inflection points in their Steam life-cycle.

\subsection{Pooled TWFE Specification}

The primary specification is a two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) event-study regression on
the full unbalanced game-month panel:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pooled}
\ln(y_{it} + 1) = \alpha_i + \gamma_t +
  \sum_{\substack{k \in [-12,\,12] \\ k \neq -1}}
  \theta_k \cdot \mathbf{1}[\tilde{k}_{it} = k]
  + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_i$ are game fixed effects, $\gamma_t$ are calendar-month fixed effects,
and $\tilde{k}_{it} = \text{clip}(k_{it},\,-12,\,12)$ is the event-time indicator
clipped to the $[-12, 12]$ window (observations outside the window are binned into the
endpoint cells). The omitted category is $k = -1$ (one month before the giveaway).
$\hat{\theta}_k$ estimates the average change in log Steam players at event-time $k$
relative to the pre-giveaway baseline, pooling first and repeat events per game.
Standard errors are clustered at the game level.

\subsection{Separated Specification: First vs.\ Repeat Giveaways}

To separate first-giveaway from repeat-giveaway effects, we add a scalar indicator
$\text{repeat\_event}_{it}$, equal to 1 in any month $t$ within 12 months after game
$i$'s \textit{second or later} giveaway:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:separated}
\ln(y_{it} + 1) = \alpha_i + \gamma_t +
  \sum_{\substack{k \in [-12,\,12] \\ k \neq -1}}
  \beta_k \cdot \mathbf{1}[\tilde{k}_{it} = k]
  \;+\;
  \delta \cdot \text{repeat\_event}_{it}
  + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
$\beta_k$ isolates the first-giveaway causal effect; $\delta$ captures the average
incremental level shift from a repeat event holding the first-event trajectory fixed.
For the 33 games with a distant repeat ($>$12 months after the first), $\text{repeat\_event}_{it}
\equiv 0$ in the event window, so $\delta$ is identified primarily by the 30 games with
a proximate repeat. For micro-batch repeats with gaps under one month (e.g., \textit{Escape
Academy} re-offered within days during the 2023--2024 holiday batch), the cluster is
collapsed to its earliest date and treated as a single event.

\subsection{Robustness Estimators}

Because treatment timing is staggered across six years, the standard TWFE estimator
is potentially biased when treatment effects are heterogeneous across cohorts
\citep{callaway2021}. We employ three complementary robustness estimators.

\textbf{LP-DiD \citep{dube2023}.} For each period $k$, LP-DiD constructs a separate
local projection using only not-yet-treated games as controls. This avoids the
contamination of treatment-effect estimates by the negative weights that TWFE assigns
to already-treated comparators under heterogeneous effects.

\textbf{Stacked DiD \citep{cengiz2019}.} Following Cengiz et al., we construct
cohort-specific stacks: for each treatment cohort (quarter of first giveaway), we
form a balanced panel containing (i) games in that cohort with event-time $k \in [-12, 12]$,
and (ii) not-yet-treated games with the same relative event window centered on that
cohort's treatment date. We stack these panels and estimate TWFE with stack (cohort)
fixed effects and event-time dummies, weighting by the inverse of the number of
stacks in which each game appears. This yields an event-study estimator that uses
only not-yet-treated controls within each stack and avoids negative-weighting bias.

\textbf{Callaway--Sant'Anna cohort ATTs \citep{callaway2021}.} We estimate
cohort-specific average treatment effects $\text{ATT}(g, k)$ for each treatment cohort
$g$ (quarter of first giveaway) and event-time $k$, using not-yet-treated games as
within-cohort controls and aggregating across cohorts weighted by cohort size. This
provides the most conservative estimate, free of any negative-weighting concern.

\subsection{Additional Robustness Checks}

\textbf{Popularity-stratified analysis.} We split the sample at the pre-treatment median
log player count (median pre-treatment avg players = 59.2) into 215 high-traffic and
214 low-traffic games and estimate Equation~\eqref{eq:pooled} separately for each half.
High-traffic games have more stable time series and cleaner pre-trend tests; this split
diagnoses whether the pooled result is driven by noise in the lower tail.

\textbf{Randomization placebo.} We permute treatment dates across all 446 games 200
times, preserving the empirical distribution of treatment months, and re-estimate the
pooled TWFE each time. If the actual $|\hat{\theta}_0|$ exceeds the 95th percentile of
the permutation null distribution, the treatment-month spike cannot be attributed to the
panel's time-series structure alone.

% ─────────────────────────── 5. RESULTS ────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main TWFE Event Study}

Figure~\ref{fig:pooled} presents the pooled TWFE event-study coefficients $\hat{\theta}_k$
with 95\% confidence intervals. The pre-period coefficients ($k \in [-12, -2]$) are
close to zero and individually insignificant for $k \leq -5$; some positive drift
appears at $k = -11$ and $k = -9$, which we discuss below. The post-period shows a
sharp, significant spike at $k = 0$ followed by rapid reversal.

Table~\ref{tab:coefs} reports the key coefficient estimates. In the giveaway month
($k = 0$), $\hat{\theta}_0 = 0.101$ (SE = 0.022, $t = 4.64$, $p < 0.001$), implying
approximately a 10.6\% increase in Steam concurrent players on the log scale. This is
the largest single-period effect. The effect remains marginally positive in $k = 1$
($\hat{\theta}_1 = 0.045$, $p = 0.047$) but turns negative and highly significant from
$k = 2$ onward. By $k = 5$, the coefficient is $-0.145$ (14.5\% below baseline); by
$k = 12$, it is $-0.267$ (23.4\% below baseline).

\subsection{Pre-Trend Test}

The joint pre-trend Wald test of $H_0: \theta_k = 0$ for $k \in [-12, -2]$ yields
$\chi^2 = 21.77$ ($df = 11$, $p = 0.026$). The marginal rejection is driven primarily
by coefficients at $k = -11$ (0.105, $p = 0.007$) and to a lesser extent $k = -9$
(0.066, $p = 0.057$)---positive drift at the far left boundary that likely reflects
boundary-bin clipping bias (all observations with $k < -12$ are coded as $k = -12$,
inflating the early bins) rather than a genuine anticipation effect. Coefficients in the
window $k \in [-5, -2]$ are all close to zero and individually insignificant, supporting
approximate parallel trends in the months immediately preceding treatment.

For the high-traffic subsample, the pre-trend Wald test yields $\chi^2 = 9.35$
($df = 11$, $p = 0.589$)---a clean failure to reject, indicating no systematic
pre-trend among the games with more stable, high-population player counts. This provides
stronger identification support for that subsample.

\subsection{Estimator Comparison: TWFE, LP-DiD, and Stacked DiD}

Figure~\ref{fig:compare} overlays the TWFE, LP-DiD, and stacked DiD \citep{cengiz2019}
event-study estimates. Table~\ref{tab:estimator_compare} summarises the key coefficients.

\textbf{Contemporaneous effect ($k=0$).} The three estimators diverge noticeably at the
treatment month. TWFE yields $\hat{\theta}_0 = 0.101$ (SE = 0.022, CI [0.058, 0.144]);
LP-DiD yields 0.131 (SE = 0.022, CI [0.088, 0.173]); stacked DiD yields 0.047
(SE = 0.008, CI [0.032, 0.063]). That TWFE and LP-DiD exceed stacked DiD is consistent
with cohort-composition bias: TWFE uses already-treated games as implicit controls,
inflating the $k = 0$ estimate, while LP-DiD restricts controls to not-yet-treated
games but estimates each period independently without the cohort-balancing that stacking
achieves. The stacked DiD, which constructs balanced cohort-specific panels and weights
each game equally across the stacks it appears in, is likely the most conservative
estimate of the contemporaneous effect.

\textbf{Speed of reversal.} The three estimators also disagree on how quickly the
initial boost dissipates. Under TWFE and LP-DiD, the $k = 1$ coefficient remains
positive (0.045 and 0.099 respectively), and the effect crosses zero only at $k = 2$
(TWFE: $-0.063$; LP-DiD: $+0.000$). Under stacked DiD, the effect is already negative
at $k = 1$ ($-0.008$, CI [$-0.028$, $0.013$]), though not significantly so, and is
statistically significantly negative by $k = 2$ ($-0.032$, CI [$-0.049$, $-0.015$]).
This faster reversal under stacked DiD is consistent with the view that LP-DiD and
TWFE are picking up a lagged positive effect from late-treated cohorts that contaminate
the $k = 1$ estimate.

\textbf{Medium-run magnitude.} All three estimators agree on a sustained decline
through $k = 12$, though they differ in scale. At $k = 10$, TWFE reaches $-0.199$
(CI [$-0.259$, $-0.138$]), LP-DiD $-0.121$ (CI [$-0.191$, $-0.051$]), and stacked
DiD $-0.164$ (CI [$-0.201$, $-0.127$]). Stacked DiD thus sits between TWFE and LP-DiD
in the medium run. The spread across estimators reflects heterogeneity in cohort
trajectories rather than uncertainty about the existence of the reversal: all three
confidence intervals are entirely below zero by $k = 5$.

\textbf{Pre-period patterns.} TWFE pre-period coefficients for $k \in [-5, -2]$ are
small and statistically insignificant (all $|t| < 0.6$), consistent with parallel
trends. LP-DiD shows a more pronounced negative pre-trend at $k = -4$ and $k = -3$
($-0.046$ and $-0.045$, marginally significant), reflecting that not-yet-treated
control games tend to be on a slight upward trajectory relative to already-treated
comparators — a drift absorbed into the fixed effects under TWFE. Stacked DiD
pre-period coefficients are generally small but show a borderline-significant positive
spike at $k = -6$ (0.060, CI [0.038, 0.083]) and $k = -7$ (0.026, CI [0.003, 0.050]).
These may reflect that games in cohorts with many not-yet-treated controls experience
elevated engagement six to seven months before treatment, possibly due to pre-announcement
media coverage, though this interpretation warrants caution.

\textbf{Overall assessment.} Despite the quantitative differences, all three estimators
produce the same qualitative two-phase pattern: a short-lived positive spike at $k = 0$
followed by a sustained and statistically significant decline below baseline through the
full $[-12, 12]$ window. The spread in the contemporaneous effect (0.047 to 0.131) is
itself informative: it bounds the plausible range of the true ATT at $k = 0$, with the
stacked DiD providing the lower bound and LP-DiD the upper bound. The robust agreement
on the reversal — which is significant at the 5\% level under all three estimators from
$k = 5$ onward — substantially strengthens the substitution interpretation.

\subsection{Separated Specification: First vs.\ Repeat Giveaways}

Figure~\ref{fig:separated} presents the separated specification (Eq.~\eqref{eq:separated}).
The repeat-giveaway coefficient is $\hat{\delta} = 0.0001$ (SE = 0.061, $t = 0.002$,
$p = 0.999$)---indistinguishable from zero. A game's second free giveaway on Epic adds
essentially no incremental Steam engagement above the first-giveaway trajectory. The
$\hat{\beta}_k$ coefficients in the separated specification are visually and numerically
very close to the pooled $\hat{\theta}_k$ coefficients, confirming that the repeat-event
contamination is negligible and the pooled specification is appropriate.

\subsection{Popularity-Stratified Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:popularity} presents event-study estimates split by pre-treatment popularity.
High-traffic games exhibit a cleaner pattern: a larger and more precisely estimated spike
at $k = 0$, flat pre-trends ($p = 0.589$), and a similarly sharp reversal. Low-traffic
games show more noise, a marginally significant pre-trend rejection ($p = 0.056$), and a
less sharp contemporaneous effect---consistent with the log transformation amplifying
proportional noise for near-zero player counts. The qualitative two-phase dynamic is
present in both strata.

\subsection{Callaway--Sant'Anna Cohort ATTs}

Figure~\ref{fig:csatt} presents the cohort-aggregated ATT estimates using not-yet-treated
controls within each treatment-quarter cohort.
The CS ATT estimate at $k = 0$ is 0.048 (SE = 0.040, CI $[-0.030, 0.126]$)---positive
in direction but not statistically significant at conventional levels. The reduced
precision relative to TWFE and LP-DiD is expected: the CS estimator conditions on narrow
within-cohort comparisons, sacrificing power in exchange for immunity to negative-weighting
bias. The post-period CS estimates turn negative from $k = 3$ onward, reaching $-0.150$
at $k = 3$ (SE = 0.056, $p < 0.01$), $-0.143$ at $k = 6$, and $-0.114$ at $k = 9$.
Despite reduced point-estimate precision at $k = 0$, all three estimators agree on the
qualitative two-phase pattern.

\subsection{Randomization Placebo}

Figure~\ref{fig:placebo} shows the distribution of $|\hat{\theta}_0|$ from 200
permutations of treatment dates alongside the actual estimate.
The permutation $p$-value is $p < 0.001$: the actual $|\hat{\theta}_0| = 0.101$ exceeds
the 95th percentile of the permutation null (0.038) by a factor of 2.7. The treatment-month
spike cannot be attributed to the panel's time-series structure or systematic calendar-time
patterns alone.

\subsection{Robustness: Clean-Sample Check}

Figure~\ref{fig:robustness} (Appendix) presents the pooled TWFE re-estimated on a
clean sample of 416 games from which all 30 games with a proximate repeat (within 12
months) are dropped entirely. The coefficients are nearly identical to the full-sample
pooled TWFE, confirming that repeat-giveaway contamination is not materially distorting
the first-event estimates.

% ─────────────────────────── 6. DISCUSSION ─────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the Contemporaneous Spike}

The $k = 0$ and $k = 1$ positive effects are consistent with the Epic giveaway
functioning as an advertising shock that temporarily lifts Steam engagement. When a
popular or cult title is announced as free on Epic, gaming media covers the announcement,
the game trends on social platforms, and players who were marginally aware of the title
are prompted to investigate. For the reasons discussed in Section~\ref{sec:lit}---Steam's
position as the primary discovery and community layer for PC games---this awareness
surge manifests as increased Steam activity: players checking the store page, reading
reviews, joining community hubs, and actively playing on Steam even if they also claim
the free Epic copy.

The magnitude of the spike (10--13\% in log players) is consistent with, though
somewhat smaller than, the 24\% effect found by \citet{clarkson2025}. The difference
may reflect sample composition: Clarkson's analysis may weight more heavily toward
high-profile titles where the advertising effect is more pronounced, whereas this
paper's sample includes 101 games with pre-treatment median players below 10---nearly
zero-engagement titles where a percentage spike is mechanically noisy and likely
attenuated in the average.

The Blood West case \citep{oshry2026}---which reported a 200\% spike in Steam
\textit{sales}---likely represents the extreme upper tail of this distribution.
Sales spikes can be larger than engagement spikes if the giveaway announcement
converts a large fraction of wishlisted-but-not-purchased users into buyers.
The present analysis measures concurrent players rather than sales, which is a
noisier but more direct proxy for actual engagement.

\subsection{Interpreting the Reversal}

The sustained reversal below baseline from $k = 2$ through $k = 12$ is the most novel
finding in this paper and warrants careful interpretation. Two mechanisms likely contribute:

\textbf{Direct substitution.} Players who claimed the free Epic copy gradually shift
their playtime from Steam to Epic. This effect is slow to materialize---it takes time
for players to actually install and play the game they claimed---which explains why
the reversal begins at $k = 2$ rather than $k = 0$. By month 3--4, the pool of
Epic-copy holders who are actively playing has grown large enough to depress Steam
concurrent counts relative to the counterfactual.

\textbf{Counterfactual trajectory.} The reversal may partially reflect that the
giveaway ``exhausts'' the marginal demand for Steam purchases. Players who were on the
verge of buying the game on Steam instead claimed it free on Epic, meaning Steam
permanently loses those players from its post-treatment baseline. Under this mechanism,
the reversal represents a permanent level shift rather than a gradual migration.

Both mechanisms are consistent with the observation that LP-DiD (which controls for
cohort-specific pre-treatment trends) shows a somewhat smaller reversal than TWFE.
This suggests that part of the TWFE reversal reflects that later-treated cohorts were
on a mild downward trajectory even absent treatment, and TWFE partially attributes
this to the giveaway effect. The LP-DiD estimate of $-0.121$ at $k = 10$ is likely
a more accurate characterization of the true reversal.

\subsection{The Null Repeat-Giveaway Effect}

The negligible $\hat{\delta} \approx 0$ for repeat giveaways has a clean interpretation:
the marginal player who has not yet claimed or played the game after the first free
promotion is a weak responder. The awareness value of a second giveaway announcement
for a title already widely known to have been free is low; players who wanted the game
already claimed it. The repeat scheduling may function primarily as a logistical catalog-
rotation decision on Epic's part, not a strategic demand-generation tool.

\subsection{Implications for Platform Strategy}

For game developers, the results suggest a nuanced calculus. The short-run advertising
benefit (10--13\% Steam player spike, real-world evidence of Steam sales spikes)
provides a positive externality from the Epic giveaway. Over the following year,
however, the migration of engaged players toward Epic reduces Steam counts below
pre-giveaway baseline. Whether this is net positive depends on: (a) the revenue
received from Epic for the giveaway deal, (b) the marginal value of the short-run
Steam spike (review volume, algorithm visibility, sales conversion), versus (c) the
long-run cost of a diminished Steam player base (fewer reviews, lower store-page
traffic). For games in active development with ongoing Steam multiplayer, the
sustained player-count erosion may be costly; for completed single-player titles, the
tradeoff may favor accepting the deal.

For Epic, the results paint a mixed picture. The giveaway program successfully
generates Steam publicity for featured games---but this publicity primarily benefits
Steam, not Epic, since the discovery activity occurs on Steam. The zero repeat-giveaway
effect further suggests that repeat promotions of the same title are not useful for
building EGS engagement. The more important question for Epic's strategy---whether the
giveaway program builds durable EGS user engagement---is not directly testable with
this data, but the evidence that the giveaway functions primarily as a Steam advertising
event suggests limited platform-building benefit.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{No never-treated group.} All 446 games are treated, so identification rests
entirely on timing variation across cohorts. If earlier cohorts (2019--2020) differ
systematically from later cohorts (2022--2025) in unobserved ways, parallel trends
is harder to defend. The Callaway--Sant'Anna estimator partially mitigates this by
avoiding already-treated comparisons.

\textbf{Player counts vs.\ purchasing behavior.} SteamCharts reports concurrent
players, not purchases or unique installs. A giveaway effect on purchases may be
larger or differently timed than the player-count effect.

\textbf{DLC and bundle giveaways.} Fifteen events distributed DLC or in-game content
rather than the base game. Mapping these to the base game's Steam App ID likely
attenuates the estimated treatment effect for those observations.

\textbf{Genre heterogeneity.} The Epic giveaway catalogue mixes single-player games
(where the migration mechanism is weaker---players play through the game once) and
multiplayer games (where platform matters continuously). A genre-stratified analysis
is left for future work due to the imprecision of the Epic dataset's free-text genre
labels.

% ─────────────────────────── 7. CONCLUSION ─────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper provides the first large-scale causal evidence on the full dynamic
trajectory of Steam player engagement following Epic Games Store free giveaways.
Using 446 games over 2018--2025 and a staggered difference-in-differences design,
we find a two-phase pattern: a significant 10--13\% increase in log Steam concurrent
players in the giveaway month, followed by a sustained reversal to 15--27\% below
pre-giveaway baseline over the following year. The contemporaneous spike is robust
to estimator choice (TWFE, LP-DiD, CS), passes a randomization placebo test
($p < 0.001$), and is not confounded by repeat-giveaway effects ($\hat{\delta} \approx 0$).

The results reconcile the advertising and substitution mechanisms that have animated
casual debate about the Epic giveaway program: both operate, but at different horizons.
The giveaway generates media awareness that temporarily lifts Steam traffic; but the
free Epic copy gradually substitutes Steam engagement over subsequent months. Taken
together, the findings suggest that Epic's free-game strategy may function more as
an inadvertent Steam advertising program than as a durable driver of Epic platform
engagement.

Future work could extend this analysis by: (1) separating single-player from
multiplayer titles to test whether the substitution mechanism is stronger in
head-to-head multiplayer titles where platform choice is more permanent; (2) studying
effects on Steam sales volume (rather than player counts) to quantify the commercial
impact more directly; and (3) examining whether the giveaway generates lasting EGS
engagement using Epic's own platform data, which is not publicly available.
