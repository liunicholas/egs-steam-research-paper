\section{Data Sources}

The analysis merges two datasets into a game-month panel. \textbf{Epic giveaway history:} A Kaggle dataset \citep{dongre2025} records 541 giveaway events (471 unique titles) from December 2018 through 2025, including start/end dates and genre labels. Sixty-three titles were given away more than once; I retain all giveaway dates to support a repeat-event analysis. \textbf{Steam player data:} For each matched title I scraped monthly average and peak concurrent player counts from SteamCharts. Matching Epic names to Steam App IDs used RapidFuzz fuzzy matching (WRatio $\geq 88$), auto-resolving 413 of 471 names; the remaining 58 were reviewed manually, leaving 463 matched titles, 8 unresolvable (5 Epic exclusives, 3 placeholders), and 13 with no SteamCharts data. The \textbf{final panel} contains 38,642 game-month observations across 446 games, covering July 2012--February 2026. Of the 446 treated games, 421 have SteamCharts data in their treatment month and 336 have a full $[-12,+12]$ observation window.

\section{Proposed Empirical Test}

I estimate a staggered difference-in-differences event study using the \textit{timing} of each game's first Epic giveaway as identifying variation. Games awaiting future giveaways serve as controls for currently treated games.

\textbf{Pooled specification.} A two-way fixed-effects (TWFE) regression with game ($\alpha_i$) and calendar-month ($\gamma_t$) fixed effects, event-time indicators binned to $[-12, 12]$:
\begin{equation}
\ln(y_{it}+1) = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{\substack{k\in[-12,12]\\k\neq-1}} \theta_k\cdot\mathbf{1}[\tilde{k}_{it}=k] + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
$\hat{\theta}_k$ estimates the average effect of an Epic giveaway on log Steam player counts, pooling first and repeat events.

\textbf{First-vs-repeat specification.} I add a scalar indicator $\text{repeat\_event}_{it}$, equal to 1 in any month within 12 months of a game's \textit{second or later} giveaway:
\begin{equation}
\ln(y_{it}+1) = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \sum_{\substack{k\in[-12,12]\\k\neq-1}} \beta_k\cdot\mathbf{1}[\tilde{k}_{it}=k] + \delta\cdot\text{repeat\_event}_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
$\beta_k$ isolates the first-giveaway effect; $\delta$ captures the incremental level shift from a repeat. Standard errors are clustered at the game level.

As a robustness check I compare TWFE estimates to LP-DiD \citep{dube2023}, which uses only not-yet-treated controls and is robust to heterogeneous treatment effects \citep{callaway2021, goodmanbacon2021}.

\section{Identification Assumptions}

\textbf{Parallel trends.} In the absence of treatment, log player counts across cohorts would have followed the same time path conditional on game and month fixed effects. This requires that Epic's curation cycle is not systematically selecting games at particular points in their lifecycle trajectories.

\textbf{No anticipation.} Player behavior does not shift before the giveaway month. Epic typically announces the following week's free game only one week ahead, making systematic pre-treatment anticipation on Steam unlikely.

\textbf{SUTVA.} A giveaway of game~$A$ does not affect Steam counts for a different game~$B$. This could fail if giveaways shift attention across competing titles in the same genre.

\textbf{Exogenous timing.} Conditional on game fixed effects, the month Epic selects a game is uncorrelated with that game's potential player-count trajectory on Steam.

\section{Testing the Assumptions}

\textbf{Pre-trend test.} Flat pre-treatment event-study coefficients $\hat{\beta}_k$ for $k < 0$, accompanied by a joint $F$-test $H_0: \beta_k=0\ \forall\, k<0$, are the primary (necessary but not sufficient) evidence for parallel trends. I also inspect within-cohort pre-trends using the Callaway--Sant'Anna estimator.

\textbf{Popularity-stratified analysis.} I split the sample at the pre-treatment median of log average players and estimate Equation~(2) separately for each half. Divergent pre-trends for low-traffic games (where measurement noise is high) would warrant caution in interpreting the pooled estimate.

\textbf{Randomization placebo.} I permute treatment dates across all 446 games 200 times and compare the actual $|\hat{\theta}_0|$ to the permutation null distribution. An observed effect above the 95th percentile rules out a spurious result driven by the panel's time-series structure.

\textbf{LP-DiD comparison.} Agreement between TWFE and LP-DiD estimates supports the internal validity of TWFE; divergence flags negative-weighting concerns.

\section{Anticipated Problems}

\textbf{No never-treated group.} All 446 games are treated, so identification rests entirely on timing variation across cohorts. If earlier cohorts (2019--2020) differ systematically from later cohorts (2022--2025) in unobserved ways, parallel trends is harder to defend. The Callaway--Sant'Anna estimator partially mitigates this by avoiding already-treated units as controls.

\textbf{Noisy low-player-count games.} 123 of 446 games have a median of fewer than 10 concurrent Steam players; the log transformation amplifies proportional noise for near-zero values.

\textbf{Repeat-giveaway contamination.} The 30 titles whose second giveaway falls within 12 months of the first create overlapping treatment windows. The scalar $\delta$ control handles this under the assumption that repeat scheduling is uncorrelated with short-run Steam trends.

\textbf{DLC/bundle mapping.} Fifteen giveaway events distributed DLC or in-game content rather than the base game, yet are matched to the base game's Steam App ID. This conflates a minor content event with a full-game giveaway and may attenuate treatment-effect estimates.

\textbf{Selection into timing.} Epic may schedule giveaways partly in response to recent media coverage that coincidentally coincides with rising Steam counts, biasing $\hat{\beta}_0$ upward.

\section{Related Literature}

\citet{clarkson2025} is the most directly related work, finding that Epic giveaways increase Steam traffic by more than 24\% and raise Steam review activity, with larger spillovers for games that leverage Steam-exclusive features. \citet{tudon2022} estimates social-multiplier effects on Steam, finding a 0.13\% own-demand increase per 1\% increase in friends' demand---a mechanism through which Epic giveaways could amplify Steam engagement by expanding the addressable player community. \citet{nabil2025} survey Steam vs.\ Epic user satisfaction and find that Steam's incumbency advantage persists despite Epic's free game program, suggesting free copies on a rival platform do not drain Steam's active player base. The methodological backbone draws on \citet{callaway2021} and \citet{goodmanbacon2021} for cohort-robust staggered DiD, and \citet{dube2023} for LP-DiD robustness checks.
